How to sit with someone who can't talk.

When the person you love has gone silent. A guide for staying in the room without asking anything of them.

Sometimes the person you love stops talking. Not just to you — to anyone. About anything. The funny family group chat goes quiet. The dinner-table conversation becomes one-sided. They are present, technically, but they are not really there.

It's tempting to interpret this as rejection. It almost never is.

The silence is usually a survival posture. When someone is in real pain — depressed, grieving, traumatised, suicidal, deeply ashamed — words can stop being possible. The act of finding a sentence, choosing it, releasing it, watching it land, anticipating the reply, formulating a response — all of that is mental work. When the system is overloaded, it shuts down at the language layer first. The person is still there. The words have just stopped reaching the surface.

You can still be there with them. Here is what that actually looks like.

Don't try to talk them out of the silence.

Don't ask three questions in a row and then read the fourth out loud as if they'll feel obliged to answer one. Don't fill the air with cheerful chatter to lift the mood. Don't say "What's wrong? You can tell me anything." even though you mean it well.

All of these are demands disguised as kindness. They make the silence feel like a failure on their part. It isn't a failure. It's a posture they have taken to stay upright.

Be in the same room without asking anything.

This is the entire move.

Sit on the same couch and read a book. Cook in the kitchen while they sit at the table. Watch a quiet film together with no requirement to discuss it. Fold laundry while they stare at the wall. The presence is the message. You are saying, with your body in the room: "You don't have to perform anything for me to stay here with you."

Make small things appear without comment.

A cup of tea on the side table. A blanket draped over the arm of the couch. A plate of toast on the counter. "I made too much, here's some." No requirement to thank you. No requirement to eat it. It is a love-letter you placed in the room and walked away from.

Use sentences that don't require a reply.

  • "I'm not going anywhere."
  • "You don't have to talk."
  • "I'll be in the kitchen if you need me."
  • "It's okay that today is hard."
  • "I love you. I'm not asking for anything back."

These are not questions and not demands. They are simply true things said aloud in a room. Some of them will land. Some won't. You're seeding the air.

Notice the difference between quiet and dangerous quiet.

Most silence is the survival kind — they are still managing, just barely. Some silence is different: flat affect, no eye contact for days, refusal of food and water, talk of being a burden or of "ending things," a strange new calmness after a long low patch, giving things away.

If you notice the dangerous kind, you have permission to widen the circle. Phone TTP. Phone SADAG. Phone a GP. Bring in a sibling, a parent, a trusted friend. You are not betraying their trust. You are keeping them alive. They will, later, be glad you did. (And if you're not sure which kind of silence it is — read Someone you love for the more detailed list.)

Look after yourself while you sit there.

Being the steady presence for someone in silent pain is its own slow exhaustion. Keep going to work. Keep eating. Have one other person you can tell the truth to. You cannot be the whole circle around them, and you shouldn't try.

The silence will lift, or it will need help to lift, or it will be the early shape of something that needs a clinician. You are not expected to know which. You are only expected to stay in the room.

The staying is the whole thing.
